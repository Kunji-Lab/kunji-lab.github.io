What is Symmetry Analysis?

Mitochondrial carriers show threefold pseudosymmetry, meaning that they have three repeats which are structurally similar but not quite identical. It is thought that two gene duplication events of a gene encoding two transmembrane alpha-helices lead to the evolution of a highly successful carrier with six transmembrane helices that could transport simple substrates. This ancestral carrier would have perfect threefold symmetry, meaning that the three repeats, and therefore domains, are identical.

However, the substrates that need to be transported are not symmetrical. Take ATP, for example. When it is in the translocation path, one part of the carrier can "see" negatively charged phosphate groups. Another part can "see" the hydrophobic adenine group. Therefore, we need to break our symmetry. For a symmetrical alanine triplet in the translocation path, a random mutation to an arginine would be favoured as it allows for an interaction with the negative phosphates. Over time, random mutations would occur which break the symmetry but allow for better binding and transport of the substrate. Therefore, the original symemtrical ancestral carrier diverges into the carrier family we know today.

By identifying residues that are particularly asymmetric, we can predict the residues involved in binding and even potentially predict the substrates transported.1

What about Symmetrical Residues?

Symmetrical residues are important too! If a triplet has maintained its symmetry over time, it implies that this is a key structural or mechanistic feature of the carrier. For example, the signature Px[DE]xx[KR] motif is responsible for the kink in the odd numbered alpha-helices2. As such, it is highly conserved and symmetric.

How can this be used?

Identifying residues likely to be involved in binding will complement and streamline wet-lab studies. Additionally, this will allow us to see similarities between carriers that are not obvious from sequence analysis. This will help with the identification of substrates for the third of carriers with no clear substrate!3

Knowledge of the binding site of a carrier will help to guide drug discovery and a deeper understanding of patient mutations.
